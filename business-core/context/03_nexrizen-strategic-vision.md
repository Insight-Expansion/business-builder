**Wei & Gabriel Perez | Living Document | Last Updated: February 2026**

## The Endgame

The ultimate goal is not to build one company and ride it to retirement. It's to become **serial builders** — founders who can repeatedly identify opportunities, build businesses, grow them, and exit profitably. The long-term progression:

1. **Build the skills** — through the current agency and law firm vertical, develop mastery across technology, business, branding, and network
2. **Achieve multiple exits** — flip several smaller businesses to build credibility, capital, and pattern recognition
3. **Transition to leverage** — use the track record and personal brand to create courses, coaching, education products, and passive income streams that don't require constant execution

This vision is deliberately vehicle-agnostic. Nexrizen's current form (AI consultancy for law firms) is a training ground, not a final destination. Every decision should be evaluated against: *does this build transferable skills and assets, or does it only serve this specific business?*

## The Four Pillars

These are the foundational capabilities that, once sufficiently developed, make the founders effective regardless of which company or product they pursue.

### Pillar 1: Technology Expertise

**Current state: Strong — this is the natural advantage.**

Both founders have deep technical backgrounds (Wei's PhD in data science from Georgia Tech, Meta experience; Gabe's engineering expertise). The team can build rapidly using modern AI tools, and staying current with technological innovation is a natural interest rather than a chore.

**What "mastery" looks like here:**
- Ability to rapidly prototype any AI/software solution within days (the "3 Days AI" approach already demonstrates this)
- Deep fluency with emerging tools (Claude Code, AI agents, automation platforms) that creates a genuine competitive moat
- Technical credibility that supports the personal brand and attracts partnerships

**Key insight from the founders' discussion:** Technology is the easiest pillar because curiosity drives continuous learning. The risk isn't falling behind — it's over-investing here at the expense of the other pillars. The team already builds well; the bottleneck is elsewhere.

**Ongoing development:**
- Stay current with Claude Code, AI agent frameworks, and automation tooling
- Build reusable systems and templates that compound across projects (this directly feeds the info product strategy)
- Develop department-specific Claude Code plugin architectures that can be productized
- Attend and present at industry conferences (AILA Texas Fall Conference, etc.) to maintain technical authority

### Pillar 2: Business Fundamentals

**Current state: Growing — this is where the most learning is needed.**

This pillar encompasses everything required to run a profitable, scalable business beyond the technical work itself.

**Sub-domains:**

**Marketing** — How to attract the right customers consistently and efficiently. This includes content marketing (LinkedIn, webinars, podcasts), paid acquisition (Meta ads, LinkedIn ads), SEO and AI-optimized search, outbound campaigns (LinkedIn DMs, email sequences), and community building (School). The current gap is heavy reliance on Upwork (manual, low-leverage) with insufficient development of scalable inbound and automated outbound channels.

**Sales** — How to convert interest into revenue. The webinar-to-consultation pipeline is promising. School community serves as a one-to-many sales environment. The shift from fixed-price to hourly billing is a critical sales discipline that protects margins. The gap is that sales is still founder-dependent (primarily Wei) with no systematized process that others can execute.

**Financial management** — How to price services, manage cash flow, understand unit economics, and make investment decisions. The fixed-price project losses were a painful but valuable lesson in pricing discipline. Understanding metrics like effective hourly rate, utilization rate, and contribution margin is essential for making sound decisions about team size, project selection, and growth investments.

**Niche selection** — How to identify markets with genuine demand, willingness to pay, and defensible positioning. The law firm / immigration vertical is well-chosen: the founders have deep domain knowledge, existing relationships, and a clear understanding of the pain points. The used car dealer exploration provided a counter-example — a "perfect market" with compressed margins and limited differentiation opportunity.

**Systems building** — How to create repeatable processes that don't depend on any single person. This includes proposal generation, client onboarding, project delivery, and most critically, lead generation and sales. The proposal builder getting faster (under 1 hour) is a good example of systems thinking applied practically.

**Exit strategy** — How to build a business that's attractive to acquirers, understand valuation drivers, and execute a clean transition. This is theoretical at this stage but becomes critical as the founders pursue their serial-builder vision.

**Key insight:** The founders recognized that being "really good at business" is a skill set that can be systematically acquired, not an innate talent. The current agency is the classroom.

### Pillar 3: Personal Brand

**Current state: Early but promising — foundation being laid with minimal effort.**

**What's working:**
- Consistent LinkedIn posting started recently and is already generating followers and impressions with near-zero time investment
- The content is establishing Wei and Gabe as practitioners (building real things) rather than just commentators
- Webinars are creating richer content that can be chopped into short-form and distributed across platforms

**What's missing or underdeveloped:**
- Content is still "max lazy" (Wei's words) — no strategic variation, no personal storytelling posts
- Personal posts that humanize the founders and create emotional connection with the audience
- Presence on platforms beyond LinkedIn (Instagram, Facebook, YouTube, podcast)
- SEO and AI-optimized search presence (website content, blog posts, search-discoverable assets)
- The audience may not yet be the *right* audience, though at this stage volume matters more than precision

**The brand vision:** Become recognized authorities at the intersection of AI technology and professional services (particularly legal). This authority supports everything else — it makes sales easier, attracts partnerships, creates distribution for info products, and eventually becomes the foundation for courses and coaching.

**Content strategy evolution:**
- **Current:** Consistent posting, mostly tactical/educational, LinkedIn-focused
- **Near-term:** Add personal storytelling posts, begin repurposing webinar content as short-form across platforms, start podcast for deeper authority building
- **Medium-term:** Develop a content engine that produces LinkedIn posts, short-form video, podcast episodes, and blog content from a single weekly effort (e.g., one webinar → 5-10 pieces of content)
- **Long-term:** The personal brand becomes a distribution channel for products, courses, and coaching — reducing dependence on paid acquisition

**Key insight from the founders' discussion:** At this stage, consistency matters more than perfection, and volume matters more than targeting. The followers may not all be "the right" followers, but building the habit and the audience base is the priority. Optimization comes later.

### Pillar 4: Network & Partnerships

**Current state: Nascent — identified as critical but underdeveloped.**

This pillar was identified during the founders' strategy session as distinct from personal brand. While brand creates broad awareness and authority, network creates specific high-value relationships that unlock opportunities no amount of marketing can generate.

**Types of network value:**

**Channel partnerships** — People or companies who can bring you deal flow. Robertas (EU reseller) is an example: if successful, he brings European clients that Nexrizen would never reach through its own marketing. This is the highest-leverage form of networking because it multiplies sales capacity without multiplying sales effort.

**Domain expertise partnerships** — People who understand a vertical deeply and can help position solutions correctly. The immigration law firm relationships serve this function already. Expanding to other legal verticals or professional services sectors would follow the same pattern.

**Technology partnerships** — Companies or individuals whose tools or platforms complement what Nexrizen builds. This could include CRM providers, legal tech platforms, or AI tool companies.

**Referral networks** — Peers, past clients, and industry contacts who send opportunities organically. This is the most sustainable form of lead generation long-term but requires consistent relationship maintenance.

**Strategic alliances** — Deeper relationships where both parties are building something together. The Sherrod O-1 visa platform discussion is an example, as is the concept of partnering with a domain expert (the "YC agency model" discussed by the founders) to deliver outcomes rather than just software.

**Key insight from the founders' discussion:** The "YC agency model" — selling outcomes rather than software — requires partnerships almost by definition. You can't sell "getting into the U.S." (Charlotte's actual need) without deep immigration domain expertise. The partnership is what transforms a software deliverable into a solution deliverable.

**Networking principles:**
- Be genuinely helpful and curious, not transactional
- Focus on a small number of high-quality relationships rather than broad shallow networking
- Every conference, webinar, and community interaction is a networking opportunity
- Partnerships should be evaluated on alignment of incentives, not just enthusiasm (the AJ/Jacob dynamic illustrates this — enthusiasm without influence is insufficient)

## Business Model Evolution

### The Spectrum from Services to Products

The founders' long-term trajectory moves along a spectrum:

**Pure services (current)** → Custom software development at hourly rates. High revenue per client, low scalability, time-for-money trade.

**Productized services (transitional)** → Standardized offerings built on repeatable templates. Example: law firm dashboard setup, consultation recording pipeline. Faster delivery, better margins, some scalability.

**Info products / plugins (emerging)** → Packaged Claude Code setups, workflow templates, department-specific plugins sold at $1-2K each. Near-zero marginal cost, highly scalable, pure profit once built. This is the most promising near-term evolution based on the founders' discussion.

**Courses and coaching (future)** → Leveraging the personal brand and track record to teach others. Requires significant authority and audience but offers the best scalability and lifestyle flexibility.

**Platform / SaaS (potential)** → Building a recurring-revenue software product. Higher investment and risk, but potentially the most valuable for exits.

The info product model deserves special attention because it sits at a sweet spot: it leverages the team's strongest pillar (technology), requires minimal ongoing effort per unit sold, and creates natural content and authority that feeds the personal brand. The proof point from the conference (a comparable seller generating $32K in two months selling GitHub repo setups at $1K each) suggests the market exists.

### The "Sell the Outcome" Principle

A recurring theme in the founders' discussion was the shift from selling software to selling outcomes. This principle applies across the entire spectrum:

- Don't sell a "consultation recording pipeline" → sell "never lose a detail from a client consultation again"
- Don't sell a "law firm dashboard" → sell "know exactly how your firm is performing in real-time"
- Don't sell a "Claude Code plugin" → sell "automate 10 hours of work per week in your practice"
- Don't sell a "sales coach AI" → sell "more closed deals and higher conversion rates"

This reframing affects pricing (outcomes are worth more than tools), marketing (outcome-focused messaging resonates more), and partnerships (domain experts help you understand and articulate the outcome).

## Guiding Principles

These emerged from the founders' conversation and diagnostic assessment:

**1. Skills over revenue.** The current business exists primarily to build the founders' capabilities across the four pillars. Revenue is necessary for survival, but optimizing for learning creates more long-term value than optimizing for short-term profit.

**2. Systems over heroics.** Any process that depends entirely on one person is a liability. Building systems (for sales, delivery, marketing, operations) is slower but creates compounding value. The retreat is a forcing function for this principle.

**3. Leverage over effort.** Prioritize activities where one unit of input creates many units of output. Content that can be repurposed, products that can be sold repeatedly, partnerships that multiply reach, and automation that eliminates manual work.

**4. Selectivity over volume.** Not every client, project, or partnership is worth pursuing. The shift to hourly-only pricing is an application of this principle. Saying no to low-margin work creates space for high-margin opportunities.

**5. Foundation before expansion.** The temptation to explore new channels, new verticals, new products, and new partnerships simultaneously is strong. But spreading too thin dilutes impact. Build a strong foundation in 1-2 areas before expanding.

**6. The vehicle is not the destination.** Nexrizen in its current form is a means to an end. Decisions should serve the founders' long-term trajectory, not just the current company's short-term metrics.

## Open Questions for Ongoing Reflection

These are strategic questions that don't have immediate answers but should be revisited regularly:

- At what point does the agency model become a ceiling rather than a vehicle? What signals would indicate it's time to stop growing the agency and go all-in on products?
- How do we evaluate partnership opportunities more rigorously? What criteria distinguish a partnership that's genuinely high-potential from one that just feels exciting?
- What would it take to make Nexrizen sellable? Even if an exit isn't imminent, building with that discipline improves the business.
- Is the law firm vertical the right long-term niche, or should the info product strategy target a broader audience? The vertical provides focus, but the Claude Code plugin concept could be much more general.
- How much personal brand building is "enough" before the returns diminish? At what point should brand-building time be redirected to product development or sales?
- What does the team look like in 12 months? Is the goal to grow the engineering team, keep it lean and add sales/marketing capacity, or move toward a solo-founder + contractors model?
